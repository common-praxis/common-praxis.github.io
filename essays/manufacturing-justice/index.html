Manufacturing Justice

Common Praxis

Discussions of justice often begin with a question of motive: are we seeking to correct past wrongs, or to prevent future harm? When the wrongs are as entrenched as colonization, slavery, and segregation, that distinction begins to blur. The question becomes not only what we owe to those who suffered but also how far justice can go in repairing the structures that keep inequality alive. Charles W. Mills, in Contract and Domination, forces us to confront how inadequate traditional theories of justice have been when faced with this reality.

John Rawls imagined a social contract built on fairness. Behind a “veil of ignorance,” where no one knows their position in society, rational individuals would choose principles that benefit everyone equally. The thought experiment is elegant. But Mills points out its fatal flaw: Rawls’ framework, shaped by the biases of white philosophers, ignores the racial hierarchies that define American and European history. It imagines fairness as if colonialism, slavery, and segregation were not the very conditions upon which modern liberal societies were built. To speak of justice without acknowledging this foundation is to construct castles in the air. Mills offers instead the notion of a “racial contract,” one that acknowledges that Black people and other marginalized groups were coerced into social systems designed for white benefit. Consent, which for Rawls secures the legitimacy of the contract, was never truly offered.

For Mills, this acknowledgment carries real political consequences. If the racial contract is our starting point, then justice cannot remain at the level of abstract principles. It must confront history directly. That confrontation, he argues, requires reparations—material efforts to redress the accumulated disadvantages created by slavery and its afterlives. Yet here the promise of his argument falters. Reparations may be necessary, but they are not sufficient.

The idea of reparations almost inevitably reduces itself to financial compensation, and while money may alleviate immediate disparities, it cannot carry the moral and structural weight Mills assigns to it. Compensation risks being treated as a transaction: a one-time payment that allows the beneficiaries of inequality to believe they have atoned, while the underlying systems remain intact. At best, it offers a symbolic gesture. At worst, it becomes an absolution, a way of closing the book on injustices that continue to shape the present.

Even if reparations were delivered generously, systemic injustice would still undermine their impact. A society built on racial hierarchy cannot be repaired by redistribution alone. Unequal education, discriminatory housing, mass incarceration, and political disenfranchisement are not solved by checks in the mail. Without dismantling these structures, financial compensation would quickly be reabsorbed into an economy designed to exploit. The result would not be transformation but repetition: money poured into a system that ensures it never delivers equality.

A society built on racial hierarchy cannot be repaired by redistribution alone.

This does not mean reparations are meaningless. They may provide recognition, a way of naming the injustice that liberal theories like Rawls’ prefer to ignore. They may empower individuals in the short term. But the deeper problem is that they do not remake the systems that perpetuate harm. Mills acknowledges the need for structural reform, yet his emphasis on reparations risks reversing the order. Structural change must come first. Only then can reparations become more than a bandage on an open wound.

Transformative justice requires us to look beyond compensation toward a full reimagining of the social, political, and economic order. It means confronting how racial hierarchy is baked into every institution: schools that reproduce segregation, housing policies that preserve inequality, voting systems that dilute Black political power. Justice cannot be merely corrective; it must be constructive, creating a society where equality is not a slogan but a lived reality.

The moral weight of reparations lies in recognition, but recognition alone does not liberate. To genuinely repair, justice must dismantle the structures that continue to harm. That work is slower, more difficult, and less dramatic than the promise of compensation, but it is the only way to prevent the cycle of injustice from repeating.

Mills is right to challenge the blindness of Rawls and the complacency of liberal philosophy. His racial contract restores history to a conversation that has too often ignored it. Yet if justice is to be more than symbolic, it cannot end with reparations. It must transform the very systems that define American life. Only then can we speak of a justice that is not temporary, not transactional, but genuinely transformative.
