Equity in Cities' AI Digital Twins

The burden of new AI technologies is to be compatible with status quo realities despite imperfect historical data.

Common Praxis

In 2026, the modern city has evolved into a complex digital architecture. Major metropolises such as Singapore, Dublin, and Riyadh have led a global shift toward digital twins; sophisticated, real-time virtual models that simulate everything from traffic flow and energy consumption to flood risks. These twins allow planners to test zoning changes or infrastructure projects in a digital sandbox before a shovel hits the ground. However, beneath these impressive 3D interfaces lies a high-stakes ethical dilemma known as the algorithmic black box. The core concern is that while these models are marketed as tools for the future, they are often trained on datasets that draw from decades of historical records. If this data is rooted in a biased past, the AI will naturally automate those same prejudices for the future.

My core argument is that we are in danger of coding systemic inequality into a city’s virtual DNA. If an AI model is trained on urban planning data from the Redlining era, a period when neighbourhoods were systematically denied investment based on race and socioeconomic status, it will view those historically Grade D areas as low-priority or high-risk today. To an algorithm focused on optimization, a neighbourhood that has lacked infrastructure for fifty years isn’t a site for urgent repair and is only a low-yield zone. When cities use AI to maximize efficiency or return on investment, the machine will almost always favour wealthy districts that already possess high property values and strong data footprints. In my view, optimization is a dangerous word in urban planning, because without human-led equity goals, it becomes a high-tech tool for further marginalizing the underserved.

To understand the scale of this problem, we must analyze how different cities handle their public transparency. Dublin has emerged as a leader in this space by treating its digital twin as a civic digital commons. Through collaborations with the ADAPT Centre, the city has pioneered an ethical governance model where residents can actually use the twin to see how cycling lanes or new buildings will impact their specific streets. By using open-source models and public data portals like Dublinked, Dublin ensures that the logic of the city is somewhat accessible. Conversely, Singapore operates with a more top-down approach. While the government uses its twin for masterful disaster planning, new 2026 amendments to the Public Sector (Governance) Act focus on sharing data with trusted partners rather than the general public. While this improves efficiency, the actual AI decision-making remains restricted, making it difficult for an average citizen to audit the specific weights used in the black box.

Meanwhile, Riyadh is serving as a fascinating ethical laboratory. In 2026, which the Saudi Cabinet officially designated as the Year of Artificial Intelligence, the city is hosting the UNESCO-sponsored International Center for AI Research and Ethics (ICAIRE). Riyadh is experimenting with sustainable-by-design models that aim to embed inclusivity directly into the system architecture. This shift moves the digital twin from a visualization tool to a proactive engine for social equity. However, even with these advancements, the global challenge remains: we cannot simply trust the math. We need a standardized social audit for urban algorithms, a process similar to the algorithmic impact assessments currently being used in Canada, to stress test these models and ensure they prioritize social health over pure financial efficiency.

The bottom line is that a digital twin is a representation of our social values. If we continue to treat these systems as neutral tools, we will find ourselves living in cities where the black box has quietly decided who gets ahead and who gets left behind. Transparency must move beyond just showing people 3D maps; it must involve revealing the recipe of the AI itself. Only by opening the box can we ensure that the smart cities of 2026 are built for everyone, rather than just the neighborhoods with the most data.
