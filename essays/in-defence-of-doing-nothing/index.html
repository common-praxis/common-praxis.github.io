In Defence of Doing Nothing

Why Specialization is a Modern Trap

Common Praxis

We’ve all heard the warning: “A jack of all trades is a master of none.” It is the ultimate shut-up tool for the curious, the line we use to discourage the student who wants to paint, code, and study law all in the same afternoon. But the modern world has a habit of editing history to suit its obsession with efficiency. The actual proverb reads: “A jack of all trades is a master of none, but oftentimes better than a master of one.”

Somewhere during the Industrial Revolution, we decided that being a cog was safer than being a connector. We began to treat specialization as the only path to mastery, neglecting the fact that the most valuable ideas rarely exist in a silo. They exist at the borders.

I’ve come to think of this interdisciplinary approach as a “Cognitive Silk Road.” When you study Philosophy, Business, and Law simultaneously, you learn to build bridges and challenge the status quo. You are learning how to apply ethical rigor to profit models while using legal frameworks to protect innovation. Specialization creates efficiency, but it also creates blind spots. The generalist, by contrast, is trained to see the whole map.

This is a strategy rooted in neuroplasticity. The brain is a muscle that thrives on novelty. When I spend my time exploring urban planning or sustainability outside of my formal degree, it operates as cross-training. By constantly forcing myself into a beginner’s mindset in new fields, I am aiming to maintain a level of cognitive agility that is often lost when we stay in one lane for too long.

We are taught that a career is a straight line, but the “One True Path” is increasingly a myth. When an industry shifts, the specialist is often stuck. The generalist, who has spent years learning how to “do everything at once,” is equipped to pivot.

Ultimately, “doing everything at once” is a commitment to interdisciplinary thinking. The most valuable ideas don’t come from knowing one thing perfectly, rather, they come from understanding the friction between ten different things.

By constantly forcing myself into a beginner’s mindset in new fields, I am aiming to maintain a level of cognitive agility that is often lost when we stay in one lane for too long.

My recommendation is to take a step back away from your lane. Take a bird’s eye view of the world and embrace systems-view thinking. The most interesting developments are happening in the overgrown grass between barriers.
