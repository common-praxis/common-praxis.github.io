Emotional Contagion vs. Moral Reason

Common Praxis

Emotions and intellect saturate our moral life. They shape not only what we believe but how we argue, how we judge, and how we act. The real question is not whether emotions and intellect coexist in morality, since they clearly do, but how they interact and what role each plays in giving our moral convictions weight. Expressivism has long suggested that moral judgment rests on feeling rather than truth, that when we say something is right or wrong we are not stating facts but voicing attitudes. It captures the immediacy of moral response: to condemn lying is not to uncover an objective property in the world but to register disapproval, much like cheering or booing at a game.

There is a certain clarity in this view. It reminds us that moral language carries heat, that behind our claims about justice or cruelty is the pulse of emotion. Yet as Susan Wolf observes in her critique, expressivism oversimplifies. It reduces moral thought to the raw expression of sentiment, ignoring the rational structures that shape, refine, and sometimes even correct those sentiments. Moral disagreement is rarely just a clash of feelings. Even when people share the same facts, they can still disagree profoundly about what justice demands. That suggests that something more than emotion is at stake.

Wolf distinguishes between simple subjectivism, which ties moral judgment directly to personal preference, and more nuanced versions such as Stevenson’s, which recognize that moral claims also function as invitations for others to feel as we do. To call racism wrong, on this view, is not only to express disapproval but to urge others to adopt the same stance. This is closer to the lived texture of moral discourse, where condemnation often seeks solidarity. Yet it still misses something essential. It does not explain why some invitations are more compelling than others, or why reasoned argument can shift our moral views. If morality is nothing but the alignment of feeling, then persuasion becomes little more than emotional contagion. Yet when we debate human rights or contest the meaning of equality, we do more than try to infect one another with moods. We reason, we justify, we attempt to show why one stance carries more weight than another.

The appeal of expressivism lies in its inclusivity. By focusing on emotion, it acknowledges pluralism and leaves room for diverse moral perspectives without demanding that they converge on a single truth. Yet Wolf insists that this inclusivity is thin. Without rational engagement, pluralism collapses into fragmentation. A society that treats moral claims as expressions of personal taste has no resources for resolving disagreement, no ground on which to critique cruelty, or to demand justice across cultural or political divides.

To dismiss reason is also to weaken the authority of moral discourse itself. Emotions may call us to act, but reason provides the framework that turns call into claim. It allows us to weigh fairness, consider consistency, and hold one another accountable. Consider debates over human rights. These are not simply clashes of feeling, but reasoned confrontations between ethical frameworks. Expressivism falters here because it denies that moral judgments can be true or false in any meaningful sense, yet without that possibility cross-cultural critique becomes nearly impossible.

Wolf’s critique points to the need for balance. Emotion gives moral discourse its urgency and power, while intellect gives it structure and coherence. Expressivism captures the immediacy of moral feeling but flattens the rational dimension that allows those feelings to be tested, debated, and revised. A richer account of morality must recognize that we live in both registers at once, feeling the force of moral concern and reasoning about its shape and justification.

Our moral lives gain their depth from the interplay of passion and reflection.

To see morality in this way is not to deny the role of emotion but to insist that it cannot stand alone. Our moral lives gain their depth from the interplay of passion and reflection. By acknowledging both, we preserve the possibility of genuine dialogue, critique, and growth, and we protect moral discourse from collapsing into either sentimentality or cold abstraction. It is only when emotion and reason work together that moral life can remain both inclusive and rigorous, capable of addressing the pluralism of our world while still sustaining the demand for justice.
