Anger and Patriotism

Common Praxis

When patriotism is peeled back from its familiar trappings of flags, anthems, and slogans, what emerges is not always a vision of unity but an economy of motives that sort, divide, and exclude. Anger lies at the heart of this economy. It can energize movements for justice by making wrongs visible and intolerable, yet it can also be bent into resentment, redirected against outsiders, and harnessed to preserve hierarchies. Nationalism in particular thrives on this redirection. It disguises exclusion in the language of belonging, turning anger into the measure of who may claim the nation and who must be cast outside it. Patriotism, often imagined as a gentle loyalty to place, becomes in practice a mechanism that licenses some grievances and silences others.

In Canada, this dynamic takes a form that is both subtle and fraught. Canadian patriotism likes to present itself as inclusive, celebratory of difference, and even morally superior to the strident nationalism of other countries. The national story centers the mosaic rather than the melting pot, the belief that diversity itself is the unifying principle. Yet beneath this polished surface, anger functions in selective and uneven ways. When it is voiced by dominant groups, it is elevated as legitimate, patriotic, and rational. When it is voiced by marginalized communities, it is often branded as irrational, disruptive, or even dangerous.

The national story centers the mosaic rather than the melting pot, the belief that diversity itself is the unifying principle.

The Freedom Convoy of 2022 provides a sharp illustration of this imbalance. At first it was framed as a protest for autonomy, a stand against government mandates, and a defense of personal freedom. Yet the deeper current that emerged was not simply about policy but about identity and belonging. Symbols of exclusion filled the streets: nationalist slogans, conspiracy rhetoric, even extremist affiliations. The anger of the convoy was received as the expression of ordinary Canadians defending their way of life, yet it narrowed freedom to a particular cultural vision. In this narrowing, it exposed the fragility of the unity that Canadian patriotism so often proclaims but rarely interrogates.

The philosopher Amia Srinivasan offers a language for making sense of this contradiction. She introduces the concept of affective injustice, which describes how emotions are validated or suppressed depending on who expresses them and against whom they are directed. In Canada, Indigenous anger over land rights, or immigrant anger over discriminatory policies, is often dismissed as excessive or uncivil. By contrast, the anger of majority groups is sanctified as patriotic, even when it carries exclusionary or reactionary content. The point is not that some emotions are naturally disruptive while others are naturally constructive, but that social hierarchies decide which emotions are recognized as rational and which are silenced.

Civility plays a central role in this sorting process. Calls for civility appear to set a neutral standard, demanding respectful discourse for all. Yet as Srinivasan argues, civility often operates as a form of containment. It forces marginalized communities to temper their anger into polite forms that neutralize its power. Anger, when properly understood, is not just an expression of hurt but a demand for recognition and accountability. To insist that it be polite is to strip it of its force, leaving injustice undisturbed. In Canadian politics, the demand for civility is used to delegitimize dissent while presenting the anger of dominant groups as authentic and patriotic.

Yet anger is not merely disruptive. It is also a moral force. James Baldwin once wrote that to be Black in America is to be in a constant state of rage, not because anger is a chosen strategy but because it is the natural response to systemic injustice. His insight applies more broadly: anger signals that something has gone profoundly wrong. It names the violation and insists on accountability. To dismiss anger as counterproductive or irrational is to deny the wrong itself. In Canada, Indigenous anger over centuries of land theft and cultural suppression is often framed as hostile rather than as the honest naming of injustice. To delegitimize such anger is to silence the very emotions that point to the unfinished work of justice.

The fragility of Canadian patriotism lies in its selective tolerance. The anger of the convoy was treated as a fight for freedom, even though it carried symbols of exclusion. The anger of marginalized communities is too often portrayed as a threat to freedom, even when it arises from exclusion itself. Patriotism in this form becomes a stage on which some grievances are amplified and others erased. Its rhetoric of unity collapses under the weight of these contradictions, revealing that belonging is never evenly distributed.

If patriotism is to mean more than sentiment, it must confront its own biases. It must recognize anger not as a threat but as a legitimate expression of moral life. Anger can be destructive when twisted into resentment, but it is also the emotion that refuses to let injustice fade quietly into acceptance. To deny its legitimacy is to weaken the very possibility of solidarity. Real unity is not built by suppressing discontent but by taking it seriously, by allowing anger to illuminate where society has failed and what must change.

The Canadian project of diversity and inclusion cannot survive if anger is permitted only for some. To mature beyond façade, patriotism must reckon with the emotions it elevates and the voices it silences. It must abandon the shallow comfort of civility that polices dissent and embrace instead the difficult solidarity that comes from listening to anger even when it is uncomfortable. Only then can patriotism transform from a shield for privilege into a genuine commitment to collective identity.

To mature beyond façade, patriotism must reckon with the emotions it elevates and the voices it silences.

A patriotism that refuses this reckoning will remain fragile, its unity forever fractured by the hierarchies it sustains. But a patriotism that accepts anger as part of justice, that recognizes it as a force of moral truth rather than a disruption to be managed, may yet evolve into something more durable. It could become not a mask for exclusion but a practice of solidarity, one that binds together not through pride alone but through the shared work of confronting injustice.
