Agency and Consciousness in Humans, Non-Human Animals, and LLMs

Why “Mindedness” marketing for LLMs gets everything supremely wrong!

Common Praxis

Artificial intelligence (AI) as a form of technology is growing increasingly rapidly. It now exists in more forms than just a few convenient large language models (LLMs), becoming embedded in many modern conveniences and even securing military defense contracts through major AI companies. In doing so, LLMs are expanding their capacity to mimic human-like cognition in their output. As this trend continues, there is an increasing tendency to associate these developments with concepts of consciousness and agency on behalf of LLMs.

However, I question that association by exploring the role of agency as a necessary condition for consciousness, analyzing how agency manifests in humans, non-human animals, and LLMs. Within this context, I argue that while humans and many animals exhibit purposeful behavior, like setting goals and adjusting their actions in response to environmental changes, LLMs lack the capacity to form intentions or check their behavior. Though they operate on large data sets and have been coded to participate in machine learning, this process does not meet the same criteria that explains genuine action in humans and animals. Thus, I argue that agency is a necessary condition for consciousness, and while humans and animals meet this threshold, LLMs fundamentally do not.

Consciousness and Attribution

To define agency is to move it away from a philosophical abstraction, and turn it more into a way in which we interpret the world around us, and in doing so, how we attribute responsibility and justify moral actions. Daniel Dennett says that when we interpret a system as if it had beliefs and desires, it is called the intentional stance. We treat the system that way because doing so helps us predict its behavior, yet success with this shortcut is not evidence that the system genuinely thinks (Dennett 1987, 15). If we accept the proposition that LLMs are conscious, we would be expected to treat them differently, in legal contexts especially. We would also need to change how we view their role in society, alongside the mental faculties of similar tools. The stakes of this debate are as much practical as they are conceptual, because positioning agency as a prerequisite for consciousness helps us better determine what kinds of ethical responses are appropriate as AI technologies rapidly evolve. At the same time, this debate intersects with broader societal anxieties, including valid concerns from widespread fear mongering about AI’s growing capacities and their limits.

My background in action explanation and agency leads me to draw on the work of Michael Thompson, a central figure in the philosophy of mind and action. In his theory of naïve action, Thompson argues that actions are intelligible within a teleological framework, which means they must be seen as directed toward some end or purpose and situated within a broader form of life that gives them explanatory meaning (Thompson 2008, 85). Within this context, agency refers to the capacity of an individual to initiate, direct, and adapt behavior in pursuit of goals, in a way that reflects internal decision-making, responsiveness to feedback, and some sort of purpose across time. For example, if I am standing on a stool reaching into a cupboard and someone asks what I’m doing, I might say I’m making bread. That response makes my current physical movements intelligible by locating them within a larger pattern of purposeful activity as I’m gathering an ingredient to do a bigger action, making bread. The action is thus explained by its relation to a broader goal that makes sense within everyday life.

As this paper will later cover in more detail, LLMs do not exhibit this kind of agency. Although their outputs may resemble purposeful behavior, they do not originate from internal goals, intentions, or evaluative processes. LLMs operate through pattern-matching based on vast training data and their responses are not actions they are taking in Thompson’s sense at all, but they are statistical continuations of prompts. They connect the input they receive with the most statistically likely outcome. As Bender et al. argue, LLMs are stochastic parrots, which basically asserts that they are systems that generate fluent text without understanding it or its purpose (Bender et al. 610).

Additionally, LLMs have no embodied or temporally unified self from which actions can arise or be sustained over time. As Metzinger explains, consciousness requires a self-model, an internal representation of the self as a coherent, goal-directed agent (Metzinger 5). LLMs do not embody a self-model because they lack the structural features required for experience, memory, or embodied orientation as they don’t have a body that interacts with the world, and therefore they have no sensory or motor feedback. Unlike humans or many animals, they do not possess any sort of subjective opinion or internal identity through which outputs could be generated with reference to its past, its future aspirations, or the changing context it exists in. This absence of purposive structure severs their behavior from meaning in the structure of the naive action theory because it is nowhere near an intelligent action, and is closest to an illusion of action produced by statistical programming.

Without agency, any behavior, despite how linguistically complex it fronts itself to be, remains conceptually dislocated from intention and meaning. This makes consciousness attribution unwarranted. As Dennett says, treating systems as if they had minds through the intentional stance may help us predict behavior but it is not evidence of genuine mentality (Dennett 15). Similarly, Metzinger emphasizes that without a self-model capable of representing its own states and goals, no system can sustain the kinds of experiential and purposive structures required for consciousness (Metzinger 6).

From a developmental standpoint, studies in human cognitive science show that even infants demonstrate early forms of agency. For example, Gopnik et al. describe how infants act purposefully in response to various stimuli and revise their actions when they come across unexpected outcomes (Gopnik et al. 2001, 98). This indicates a capacity for belief revision and goal-directed experimentation.

Philosophically, this evidence supports Thompson’s theory that human actions are intelligible only within a teleological structure, where actions are seen as directed toward purposes and embedded in a broader pattern of life. The infant’s capacity to respond and revise becomes meaningful when interpreted within this purposive framework. Therefore, human agency can be understood as involving continuity, normative structure, and flexibility, all of which contribute to a temporally extended understanding of action. These features make human beings a clear paradigm case of what it means to be an agent and a candidate for consciousness.

While non-human animals may lack the linguistic or abstract reasoning capacities of humans, many can still show some traits of agency. For example, chimpanzees have been observed using various tools, even crafting some for fishing and nut cracking (Boesch and Boesch 1990, 90). This leads us to see that perhaps animals have internal representations of problems and some means to an end type of reasoning. Additionally, crows have solved multi-step problems using sequential tools, showing an understanding of causal structure and planning (Taylor et al. 2007, 125). Orangutans and ravens have even demonstrated future-oriented planning, selecting tools hours before using them (Osvath 2009, R190). These behaviors are goal directed, contextual, and responsive to feedback, all frameworks accepted as pointing towards agency. Although animal agency may differ in degree from that of humans, it still involves initiating action, responding to new information, and acting in accordance with internal states. They act within a form of life, and that structure is sufficient to consider them as agents and potentially conscious.

In contrast, LLMs like ChatGPT do not act as their outputs are generated by statistical models trained on massive pre-existing information with no internal system of evaluation or intention (Bender et al. 610). Unlike humans and animals, they do not show any independent reflective self-correction. As mentioned earlier, Dennett’s intentional stance is merely instrumental and does not imply that the system actually has mental states (Dennett 1987, 20). Metzinger reinforces this by arguing that without a body or perspective, LLMs cannot possess even any minimally conclusive idea of agency (Metzinger 2018, 6). While their linguistic capacity is large, it is due to the fact that they have been programmed to create products out of pattern recognition and completion. Nothing about their output is meaningful or situated in a broader theory of life. They do not know what their words mean, and when their user generates political insight, they have no connection to who these actors are in the real world, and thus no stakes. Thus, we may accept that LLMs simulate conversation and various dialogues as a user requests it to do so, but they do not initiate or evaluate behavior. The evidence reviewed supports my thesis because it shows that humans and animals exhibit goals in their actions as well as behaviour that changes as contexts change, whereas LLMs exhibit none of these. This asserts that agency is both absent in LLMs, present in animals and humans, and necessary for consciousness.

Agency is not an on off switch. Some new mobile robots combine reinforcement learning with limited memory and can perform simple self revisions. For instance, Kober, Bagnell, and Peters describe robots that learn and update their own movement policies while interacting with the physical world, using past experience to guide future actions (Kober et al. 2013, 1240). These systems suggest a scale that ranges from minimal agency to rich agency. They remain far below the standard seen in animals, yet they show how future AI might inch closer. Placing LLMs on this scale reveals that text only models still sit near the bottom because they lack embodiment and interactive feedback.

Agency is not an on-off switch.

The comparison reveals that humans and animals act within forms of life, in ways that are purposive and evaluable while LLMs do not because their behavior is fragmented and ungrounded. Without agency, there is no anchor for consciousness. However, one might argue from a functionalist view that what matters is a behavioral role, not structure. But functionalism often neglects the embodied, purposive dimensions of mind that give rise to subjective experience, essential for the practical ways in which we view agency, sentience, and consciousness.

Some might say that LLMs create responses that look like they engage in planning. Could that be taken as a form of action, even if they physically cannot do so? My answer is that pretending you will do something is not the same as carrying out those plans in the right context, as necessary. A real agent strings actions together and revises course when the world changes unexpectedly, despite the original actions. LLMs generate fluent sequences because they sit on statistical pattern-matching engines and the appearance of a plan is the most likely continuation of a prompt and it does not track out to be an internally sparked project. They launch nothing and evaluate nothing, so the performative shadow of planning never crosses the line into initiation (Bender et al. 2021, 611).

A second worry may ask why we grant consciousness to animals with fairly modest cognitive toolkits yet deny the same courtesy to advanced AI. I believe this comparison misses a crucial ingredient, which is feedback-driven embodiment. Even a crow or a chimp interacts with the material world through a sensorimotor loop, updating its behavior when a stick snaps or their food is harder to find than expected. That loop grounds learning and correction, which are capacities documented in ethology (Taylor et al. 2007, 126; Osvath 2009, R190). Unlike them, LLMs float in bodilessly, and as a result, they register no surprises and therefore do not possess the evaluative processes that convert behavior into agency.

Finally, some accuse this standard of being more comfortable in anthropocentric conclusions, which are another way of saying that we are biased towards this view so that humans look to be the superior category in this comparison. Yet the criteria I and countless others offer are species-neutral, and not subversive to human centric ideals at all. A minimal self-model that sustains goals over time and anchors experience is a framework that plenty of non-human creatures clear, while current text-based AIs don’t. Without a persistent first-person framework, there is no someone for consciousness to inhabit (Metzinger 2018, 6). The ideas and frameworks shared prior therefore exclude LLMs not because they’re non-human, but because they lack the structural prerequisites shared by all known conscious beings.

I have therefore argued that agency is a necessary condition for consciousness. While humans and some animals demonstrate this capacity, LLMs lack the features to qualify as agents. Future research might consider whether AIs with memory, embodiment, and feedback loops could become closer to agency, but for now, attributing consciousness to LLMs based on language use is unjustified.
