Thermal Inequity: Mapping the Relationship Between Tree Canopy and Wealth

If you want to see where the highest temperatures are, don't look at a thermometer; look at a map of household income.

Common Praxis

While dramatic hurricanes and wildfires dominate the news cycle, the deadliest climate-related killer is a silent one: extreme heat. However, heat does not act as a great equalizer across the urban landscape. In cities globally, temperature is often a reflection of historical disinvestment and current economic status. If one overlays a thermal heat map, derived from satellite data like Landsat via Google Earth Engine, with a map of median household income, the correlation is quite precise. Wealthier neighbourhoods are consistently defined by green landscapes, shielded by mature oak and maple canopies that provide natural cooling through shade and evapotranspiration. In contrast, lower-income neighbourhoods, often shaped by decades of discriminatory redlining and industrial zoning, are grey landscapes dominated by asphalt and dark rooftops. These surfaces possess high thermal mass, soaking up solar radiation throughout the day and radiating it back into the atmosphere at night, creating a localized furnace effect.

This disparity moves the conversation beyond environmental aesthetics into the realm of thermal justice. Access to a livable temperature should be recognized as a fundamental human right rather than a luxury tied to a specific zip code. Residents in heat-stressed neighbourhoods face a compounding burden: they suffer from higher rates of heatstroke and cardiovascular stress, yet they are often the least able to afford the skyrocketing utility bills required to run air conditioning. To bridge this heat gap, cities must treat green infrastructure, such as bioswales, urban forests, and reflective cool roofs, as essential public health infrastructure. This planning is about correcting a spatial injustice that leaves the most vulnerable populations exposed to the most lethal elements of a warming planet.

However, the path to cooling our cities is complicated by the risk of Green Gentrification. There is a documented paradox where the introduction of high-end green spaces into marginalized areas can spike property values, effectively pricing out the very residents the intervention was meant to protect. To avoid this, thermal justice must be paired with anti-displacement policies, such as rent protections and community land trusts. The goal should be a distributed network of shade that prioritizes the hottest blocks first, ensuring that greening a neighbourhood does not mean erasing its community. By centring urban forestry in the neighbourhoods that need it most, we can transform our cities from thermal traps into resilient, equitable habitats where the cooling power of a tree canopy is available to everyone, regardless of their income.
